Yiyi Zhang

Economy


I’m a seasoned economist with a passion for understanding and navigating the intricacies of market forces, labor economics, and the impact of policy on both individuals and organizations.

My journey in economics began with a deep curiosity about how the invisible hand shapes everything from the price of goods to the structure of entire industries. Over the years, I’ve been fortunate to turn that curiosity into a rewarding career, allowing me to work on a wide range of topics from minimum wage dynamics and labor market fluctuations to gift economies and collective action challenges. Each project has added depth to my perspective on how economic theories play out in real-world settings, and I’m always looking for ways to connect academic insights with practical solutions. Professionally, I’m driven by a commitment to research that is as rigorous as it is relevant. I currently work in an applied economics role, where I analyze complex data sets, interpret findings, and present them in a way that can inform decision-making at various levels. Recently, my focus has been on labor markets, studying the effects of minimum wage adjustments, and investigating collective goods and the mechanisms that help overcome collective action problems. I aim to contribute not only to the academic discourse but also to provide actionable insights for policymakers, businesses, and communities. My approach is detail-oriented and collaborative, as I firmly believe that the best solutions come from blending diverse viewpoints and interdisciplinary approaches. In addition to my research work, I’m dedicated to fostering clear and accessible economic communication. Whether it’s through teaching, writing, or presenting findings to a non-specialist audience, I strive to demystify complex concepts and make economics relatable and engaging. I also mentor younger professionals and students interested in economics, helping them build strong analytical skills and a balanced perspective that considers both quantitative rigor and social impact. This mentorship has been one of the most rewarding aspects of my career, as it allows me to see the next generation of economists develop their own passion and expertise. Outside of work, I’m deeply interested in the dynamics of gift-giving and its role in primitive institutions, particularly as it relates to themes like unilateral obligation and insurance mechanisms. I find that studying these areas not only enriches my understanding of economic anthropology but also provides valuable insights into the social and cultural dimensions of economic interactions. I’m an advocate for continuous learning, always on the lookout for new ways to expand my knowledge and bring fresh perspectives to my work. Ultimately, my goal is to contribute to an economics that is as thoughtful and inclusive as it is precise and analytical, bridging the gap between theory and practice for a more informed and equitable world.
